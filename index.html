Works in Progress

Laptop Jam

A collaborative music making experience. The idea of this project was to let anybody participate in a kind of “jam session,” being able sit down and make music with friends (or strangers) without the barriers of skill and virtuosity that ordinary instruments present.

Each musician is given a laptop and an interface with two parts. First, they are given a simple “primary” instrument – for example, a bass controlled by pressing keys on a keyboard or a marimba-like instrument controlled through a GUI sequencer. Instruments are designed with some restraints (e.g. some are restricted to certain notes in a scale) so that it’s harder to “make mistakes,” but they still give plenty of room for creative freedom. In theory, this allows the musicians to focus only on making musical decisions. The interfaces themselves are also minimal and instructions are intentionally vague, letting users freely explore and develop their own techniques.

At the same time, musicians have the ability to control the parameters of another user’s instrument. For example, moving the mouse to one end of the screen might change the timbre of another instrument or pan it across the stereo field. In addition to the extra attention that this second task demands, it encourages individuals to engage with and respond to the musicians around them, rather than going off independently.

As the work progresses, different instruments are automated to phase in and out as musicians experiment with new ideas. The result is a naturally evolving ambient-like piece, (mostly) free of traditional musical structure but still easy (and pleasant) to listen to.

The idea started as my final project last fall for MUSI 425 – Special Topics in Music, Multimedia Art, and Technology and utilized Max/MSP, Ableton Live, and Max for Live all networked via OSC. The excerpt showcased here is from my in-class presentation, performed and completely improvised by three of my classmates who have never used (or heard) the work before.*

Hopefully, this year I can continue refining it and adding new instruments, interfaces, and features.

*I apologize for the suboptimal audio – it was recorded from a camera filming the presentation, so some room chatter is audible.

Other Work

Cross Campus

Last spring, I served as a Producer and Lead Sound Engineer on the Yale Daily News’ first-ever podcast, Cross Campus, where my work included planning episodes, recording interviews, and assembling and editing episode content, among other tasks. Because the team and I had little background in podcasting before Cross Campus, figuring out how to put together episodes was both daunting and exciting. In the process, I learned a lot about thoughtful sound design the importance of good pacing in storytelling, skills which I think carry over to many different areas of my work.

The project proved so successful that after only a single semester, the YDN received large donations to build a brand-new studio space just for podcast production.
